# ADR 0008: Tiered brand catalog for quality and global coverage

- Status: Accepted
- Date: 2026-07-10

## Context

A repository containing one reviewed directory per brand is appropriate for transparent, high-confidence records, but it cannot by itself support global long-tail discovery at tens of millions of domains. Open Brands needs both strong reviewed data and broad automated coverage without presenting inferred or observed data as verified fact.

## Decision

Open Brands uses three explicitly separated catalog tiers.

### 1. Reviewed registry

The reviewed registry is the highest-trust layer and remains in git under `data/brands/<brand-id>/`.

It contains:

- reviewed logos, symbols, wordmarks, lockups, palettes, fonts, and brand metadata;
- field-level provenance and review status;
- historical identity records and redirects;
- human-reviewed and brand-owner-supported corrections;
- assets approved for public Open Brands delivery.

The public website, package, and APIs clearly label this tier as `reviewed` or `verified`, depending on its evidence.

### 2. Claimed profiles

A claimed profile is controlled by a person who proves authority over a brand through an accepted verification method, such as domain DNS, a domain email challenge, a verified public-site token, or a documented manual review.

Claims are append-only audited events. A claim proves control of the profile; it does not automatically prove that every submitted factual statement or asset may be redistributed.

Claimed changes that add public artwork or alter reviewed registry data still pass the canonical validation and review pipeline. Claim records and verification evidence are stored in the claims service and exported into immutable dataset releases. Private verification evidence is never published.

### 3. Observed catalog

The observed catalog provides broad domain and organization coverage from public first-party surfaces and permitted public datasets.

Observed records may contain:

- domain and organization identity;
- discovered names and aliases;
- public favicons and candidate identity assets;
- social profile links;
- inferred colors, fonts, location, industry, and descriptions;
- source URLs, observation timestamps, checksums, and confidence signals.

Observed records are generated by a reproducible ingestion pipeline and stored as immutable, partitioned dataset artifacts in R2. They are indexed in a dedicated catalog search service behind a provider-neutral interface. D1 remains operational storage and is not used for the global search catalog.

Observed records are never committed as millions of individual git directories and never receive `verified` status automatically. Candidate assets are not promoted into the reviewed public asset CDN until they pass the asset, provenance, and legal policy.

## Resolution and precedence

When multiple tiers contain a field, precedence is:

1. current reviewed registry value;
2. current validated claimed value;
3. latest sufficiently confident observed value;
4. `null`.

Every public field includes or can resolve to:

- tier;
- confidence/review state;
- source references;
- first observed date;
- last observed or verified date;
- replacement or historical state where relevant.

Consumers may request a minimum trust tier. Reviewed-only package exports never silently include observed values.

## Storage and indexing

- Git stores the reviewed registry, taxonomies, schemas, accepted policy, and deterministic tooling.
- R2 stores versioned catalog shards, approved assets, generated derivatives, release manifests, bulk exports, and observed evidence snapshots.
- A scalable external search/index engine stores the query index for the observed catalog. The implementation must remain replaceable and be selected by benchmark rather than preference.
- D1 stores operational workflow state, not the global catalog.
- Dataset releases pin reviewed, claimed, and observed component versions independently and expose a combined release manifest.

## Ingestion rules

The observed pipeline may use official websites, public managed social profiles, public registries, and datasets whose terms permit the intended processing and redistribution.

It must not:

- ingest or copy Brandfetch API responses, CDN assets, pages at scale, or other restricted competitor data;
- treat generated descriptions or inferred context as first-party statements;
- bypass robots, authentication, paywalls, access controls, or redistribution restrictions;
- publish unsafe or unreviewed SVG markup;
- convert low-confidence observation into a reviewed default.

## Scale milestones

Coverage targets are measured as successfully resolved independent public domains, not inflated aliases or duplicate hostnames.

1. 10,000 high-value reviewed brands.
2. 1 million observed domains with source and freshness metadata.
3. 10 million observed domains with production search and update pipelines.
4. 50 million-plus observed domains with published quality methodology.
5. Continued expansion beyond competitor-reported coverage only when measured quality does not regress.

No public coverage claim is made without a reproducible measurement report.

## Consequences

- Open Brands can remain transparent and high-quality while scaling far beyond a manually curated registry.
- The website and API must always communicate trust tier and provenance.
- Search, API, package, and bulk delivery require explicit reviewed-only and combined-catalog modes.
- The observed catalog introduces a new ingestion and indexing subsystem, but it does not weaken reviewed registry guarantees.
- The product can outperform closed providers on transparency, caching, reproducibility, historical data, and local/offline use while competing on breadth.
